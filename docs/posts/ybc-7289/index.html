    YBC 7289

    YBC 7289

    1 May 2024
    3D Printing
    3D Builder, Blender, Ultimaker

    Recently, I wanted to learn how to use a 3D Printer after having not had the opportunity to do so for quite some time. There were many reasons why: the cost of buying one, the lack of space to store one, not knowing which one to get, and not knowing what I’d even do with one. However, I was able to gain access to a very good one without having to worry about most of the aforementioned reasons, except the last point: what should I print?

    On one of my occasional Wikipedia crawls, I read into the fascinating artefact known as YBC 7289, a Babylonian clay tablet that provides an accurate approximation of \(\sqrt{2}\) , the length of the diagonal of a unit square, to the equivalent of six decimal places. When I discovered that there exists a digitised version of the tablet suitable for 3D printing, I kept it bookmarked with the intention of eventually printing one, to scale, for a friend of mine whom I believe would value it greatly.

    Figure 1: YBC 7289 obverse (left) and reverse (right). Adapted from: Wikimedia Commons.

    The 3D Printer available to me was the Ultimaker S3. In order to make the 3D model suitable for this printer, I did the following:

    1. Download the .glb version of the digitised tablet from the link above.
    2. Import the .glb into Blender.
    3. In Blender, select the entire object with A and Join all of them together to make a single object.
    4. Scale the dimensions of the object so that its width is 8cm, which would make it approximately to scale.
    5. Save in the .stl format.
    6. Open the .stl file in Microsoft’s 3D Builder.
    7. Repair the object (if 3D Builder states that it needs to be repaired, which it did for me).
    8. Save the repaired object as .stl.
    9. Load the repaired object into Ultimaker Cura.
    10. Configure printing options and select Slice and save the resulting .ufp to a pen drive.
    11. Configure the Ultimaker S3 as needed.
    12. Insert the pen drive into the Ultimaker S3, select the .ufp, and start the scan.

    Figure 2: Left, the 3D model in Ultimaker Cura; Right, the printed result.

    The print took approximately 9 hours and produced a nicely detailed result where you can really see and feel the cuneiform marks. However, my inexperience led to a couple of issues that I will definitely avoid in the future.

    Firstly, I should have rotated the model in Cura to reduce amount of support material needed to print the tablet. As shown in Figure 2, there is a lot of support material because the bottom of the tablet has a large gap in it. The orientation I printed at also caused many nooks and crannies to be filled with support, which it would never have needed at a different orientation.

    Figure 3: The tablet after chipping away as much of the Breakaway material as possible. (It was a tricky process that led to my chisel slipping many times and causing fingers to bleed, hence the blue tissue paper.)

    Secondly, I would swap the support material from Breakaway to PVA because Breakaway cannot simply be washed off the tablet like PVA can. Instead, it requires chipping away by hand, which is incredibly difficult to do completely because of the awkward shape of the tablet, as can be seen at the bottom of Figure 3. I think it was important to practise with designs that I would want to keep, regardless of how shoddy the outcome may be, instead of printing some simple, throw-away cubes and pyramids. I’ll continue practising with other digitised, priceless artefacts with which to clutter my bookshelf :-)
